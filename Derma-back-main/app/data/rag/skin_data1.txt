================================================================================
DISEASE #1: PSORIASIS
Classification: Chronic Autoimmune Inflammatory Disease
================================================================================

OVERVIEW:
Psoriasis is a chronic autoimmune inflammatory disease affecting the skin, nails, and joints. It results from immune system dysregulation leading to excessive skin cell proliferation. Not contagious but lifelong condition associated with systemic inflammation and multiple comorbidities.

KEY FACTS:
- Not contagious
- Chronic (lifelong)
- Associated with systemic inflammation
- Linked to other diseases (comorbidities)
- Genetic predisposition with environmental triggers

SYMPTOMS - SKIN:
- Red plaques with silvery scales
- Dry, cracked skin
- Itching and burning sensation
- Well-demarcated lesions

SYMPTOMS - NAILS:
- Nail pitting
- Thickening of nails
- Yellow discoloration
- Onycholysis (nail separation)

SYMPTOMS - JOINTS:
- Pain and stiffness
- Joint swelling
- Psoriatic arthritis

SEVERITY CLASSIFICATION:
- Mild: <3% body surface area (BSA)
- Moderate: 3-10% BSA
- Severe: >10% BSA or sensitive areas involved

CAUSES & ETIOLOGY:
- Autoimmune dysfunction (T-cell mediated)
- Genetic susceptibility (HLA genes)
- Environmental interaction
- Multifactorial inheritance pattern

MOLECULAR MECHANISM:
- T-cell activation and infiltration
- Cytokine release: TNF-alpha, IL-17, IL-23
- Increased keratinocyte proliferation
- Accelerated turnover: 3-5 days (vs normal 28 days)
- Inflammatory cascade amplification

TRIGGERS & RISK FACTORS:
- Psychological stress
- Acute/chronic infections (streptococcal, viral)
- Medications (beta-blockers, NSAIDs, lithium)
- Trauma (Koebner phenomenon)
- Smoking
- Alcohol consumption
- Cold weather exposure

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS:
- Eczema (atopic dermatitis)
- Seborrheic dermatitis
- Fungal infections
- Lichen planus
- Drug eruptions

DIAGNOSIS METHODS:
- Clinical evaluation and morphology
- PASI score (Psoriasis Area and Severity Index)
- Skin biopsy (histopathology when needed)

MONITORING & FOLLOW-UP:
- Regular liver/kidney function tests (systemic drugs)
- Joint evaluation (psoriatic arthritis assessment)
- Cardiovascular risk assessment
- Mental health screening

COMPLICATIONS:
- Psoriatic arthritis
- Cardiovascular disease (increased risk)
- Diabetes mellitus
- Depression and anxiety
- Metabolic syndrome

PATIENT MANAGEMENT ADVICE:
- Daily skin moisturization
- Identify and avoid personal triggers
- Maintain healthy body weight
- Stress management techniques
- Regular exercise

RED FLAGS - EMERGENCY CARE REQUIRED:
- Erythrodermic psoriasis (whole body redness)
- Severe pain with fever
- Pustular psoriasis with systemic symptoms

================================================================================
DISEASE #2: TINEA INFECTION (DERMATOPHYTOSIS / RINGWORM)
Classification: Contagious Fungal Infection (Non-parasitic)
================================================================================

OVERVIEW:
Tinea infections are common fungal infections caused by dermatophytes that affect the skin, hair, and nails. These fungi feed on keratin and thrive in warm, moist environments. Despite the name "ringworm," no parasitic worms are involved. Highly contagious through direct contact and contaminated objects.

KEY FACTS:
- Contagious fungal infection (not bacterial, not parasitic)
- Caused by dermatophyte fungi
- Affects skin, scalp, and nails
- Spreads easily in humid, warm conditions
- Common in athletes and crowded environments

SYMPTOMS - SKIN LESIONS:
- Red, ring-shaped rash with raised edges
- Itching and irritation
- Scaling and dryness
- Central clearing with erythematous border

SYMPTOMS - FEET (TINEA PEDIS):
- Peeling skin between toes (interdigital)
- Burning sensation and pain
- Cracks and blisters
- Maceration of skin

SYMPTOMS - GROIN (TINEA CRURIS):
- Red itchy rash in inner thighs
- Burning and discomfort
- Possible satellite lesions
- Sharp, well-demarcated borders

SYMPTOMS - SCALP (TINEA CAPITIS):
- Scaly patches on scalp
- Hair loss (alopecia)
- Black dots appearance (broken hairs)
- Swelling in severe cases (kerion formation)
- Pustules and drainage

SYMPTOMS - NAILS (TINEA UNGUIUM):
- Thickened nails (hyperkeratosis)
- Yellow or discolored nails
- Brittle or deformed nails
- Nail destruction

SEVERITY CLASSIFICATION:
- Mild: Localized skin infection, single area
- Moderate: Multiple areas involved
- Severe: Nail involvement OR scalp involvement OR widespread lesions

CAUSATIVE ORGANISMS:
- Trichophyton species (most common)
- Microsporum species
- Epidermophyton floccosum

MOLECULAR MECHANISM:
- Fungal invasion of keratinized tissue
- Keratinase enzyme production (breaks down keratin)
- Colonization of outer skin layers (stratum corneum)
- Inflammatory response at host-fungal interface
- No deep tissue invasion (superficial)

TRANSMISSION ROUTES:
- Direct skin-to-skin contact with infected person
- Contact with infected animals (zoonotic)
- Contaminated objects: towels, combs, floors, shoes
- Shared spaces: pools, gyms, locker rooms

TRIGGERS & RISK FACTORS:
- Warm, humid climate exposure
- Excessive sweating (hyperhidrosis)
- Poor hygiene practices
- Tight clothing (reduced ventilation)
- Immunosuppression
- Shared public spaces
- Athlete population (sports-related)

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS:
- Eczema (atopic dermatitis)
- Psoriasis
- Contact dermatitis
- Candidiasis (yeast infection)
- Seborrheic dermatitis

DIAGNOSIS METHODS:
- Clinical examination and morphology
- KOH test (Potassium Hydroxide microscopy)
- Fungal culture (gold standard)
- Wood's lamp examination (some species)

MONITORING & FOLLOW-UP:
- Monitor for treatment response
- Check treatment adherence
- Nail infections require long-term follow-up (6-12 months)
- Assess for recurrence

COMPLICATIONS:
- Secondary bacterial infection (cellulitis risk)
- Chronic/recurrent infection
- Scarring (rare)
- Permanent hair loss (severe scalp infection)
- Nail destruction and dystrophy

PATIENT MANAGEMENT ADVICE:
- Keep skin dry and clean
- Do not share personal items (towels, combs, razors)
- Wear breathable clothing
- Change socks regularly (moisture control)
- Treat infected pets
- Maintain good hygiene

RED FLAGS - EMERGENCY CARE REQUIRED:
- Severe scalp swelling (kerion with drainage)
- Spreading infection despite treatment
- Signs of bacterial superinfection (pus, fever)
- Systemic symptoms (fever, lymphadenopathy)

================================================================================
DISEASE #3: URTICARIA (HIVES)
Classification: Acute/Chronic Hypersensitivity Reaction
================================================================================

OVERVIEW:
Urticaria is a skin condition characterized by itchy, raised welts (hives) that can appear anywhere on the body. Often triggered by allergic reactions, physical stimuli, infections, or may occur without identifiable cause (idiopathic). Can be acute or chronic with variable presentation and prognosis.

KEY FACTS:
- Common skin condition (affects 15-20% of population lifetime)
- Acute form: <6 weeks duration
- Chronic form: >6 weeks duration
- Often allergic or idiopathic etiology
- May be associated with angioedema (deep swelling)
- Usually not life-threatening except severe anaphylaxis cases

SYMPTOMS - SKIN MANIFESTATIONS:
- Raised red or skin-colored welts (wheals/hives)
- Intense itching, burning, or stinging sensation
- Variable shapes: round, ring-like, irregular
- Rapid appearance and disappearance (<24 hours per lesion)
- Evanescent nature of individual lesions

SYMPTOMS - ANGIOEDEMA (DEEP SWELLING):
- Deep swelling under the skin (dermis/subcutaneous)
- Common sites: lips, face, eyelids, hands, feet
- May involve throat (potentially dangerous)
- Painful rather than itchy
- Can last 24-72 hours

URTICARIA TYPES:
- Acute urticaria
- Chronic spontaneous urticaria
- Inducible urticaria: cold, heat, pressure, sunlight, exercise

CAUSES & ETIOLOGY:
- Allergic reactions: foods, medications, insect stings
- Physical triggers: cold, heat, pressure, sunlight
- Infections: viral or bacterial
- Autoimmune factors
- Stress and hormonal changes
- Medications: NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, aspirin
- Idiopathic (unknown cause - 50% of chronic cases)

MOLECULAR MECHANISM:
- Mast cell activation and degranulation
- Histamine release (primary mediator)
- Increased vascular permeability
- Dermal edema formation
- Local inflammatory response
- Complement activation (rare)

TRIGGERS & PRECIPITATING FACTORS:
- Foods: nuts, shellfish, berries
- Drugs: NSAIDs, antibiotics, statins
- Psychological stress
- Temperature changes
- Physical exercise
- Viral/bacterial infections
- Food additives and preservatives

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS:
- Contact dermatitis
- Atopic dermatitis (eczema)
- Drug eruptions
- Urticarial vasculitis
- Erythema multiforme
- Mastocytosis

DIAGNOSIS METHODS:
- Clinical evaluation and patient history
- Trigger identification (food/contact diary)
- Allergy testing (skin prick, serum-specific IgE) if indicated
- Blood tests for chronic cases (CBC, CMP, thyroid)

MONITORING & FOLLOW-UP:
- Track symptom frequency and severity
- Monitor response to antihistamines
- Identify and document triggers
- Assess impact on quality of life

COMPLICATIONS:
- Progression to chronic urticaria
- Angioedema with respiratory involvement
- Sleep disturbance
- Significant quality of life reduction
- Anaphylaxis (rare but life-threatening)

PATIENT MANAGEMENT ADVICE:
- Identify and avoid known triggers
- Take antihistamines regularly (not just as needed)
- Stress management and relaxation
- Keep detailed symptom/trigger diary
- Avoid hot baths (can trigger flares)

RED FLAGS - EMERGENCY CARE REQUIRED:
- Swelling of throat or tongue (airway risk)
- Difficulty breathing or stridor
- Severe angioedema
- Signs of anaphylaxis (hypotension, shock)
- Continuous symptoms despite treatment

DISEASE #4: LICHEN PLANUS
Classification: Chronic Immune-Mediated Inflammatory Disease
================================================================================

OVERVIEW:
Lichen planus is a chronic inflammatory disease affecting the skin, hair, nails, and mucous membranes (oral and genital areas). Characterized by distinctive purple, shiny, flat-topped papules on skin and lacy white patches on mucosal surfaces. Immune-mediated condition with variable course; may be self-limited or chronic. Not contagious.

KEY FACTS:
- Chronic inflammatory disorder (immune-mediated)
- Affects skin, mucosa, nails, and hair
- Autoimmune-like pathophysiology
- Not contagious
- May be self-limited or persist years
- Associated with Hepatitis C in some populations

SYMPTOMS - SKIN LESIONS:
- Purple, polygonal, flat-topped papules
- Shiny surface with fine white lines (Wickham striae)
- Common sites: wrists, forearms, ankles, shins
- Intense itching
- Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation
- Erosive variants may form

SYMPTOMS - ORAL LICHEN PLANUS:
- Lacy white patches on tongue or inner cheeks (reticular form)
- Painful ulcers or erosions
- Burning sensation during eating
- Erosive form most symptomatic
- Difficulty with certain foods

SYMPTOMS - GENITAL LICHEN PLANUS:
- Painful erosions or ulcers
- Discomfort during intercourse (dyspareunia)
- Possible scarring (erosive variant)
- Erosions may lead to adhesions

SYMPTOMS - HAIR/SCALP (LICHEN PLANOPILARIS):
- Hair loss (scarring alopecia)
- Scalp inflammation
- Irreversible hair follicle destruction
- Permanent baldness in affected areas

SYMPTOMS - NAILS:
- Longitudinal ridging and grooves
- Nail thinning or splitting
- Nail loss in severe cases
- Pterygium unguis (nail-skin fusion)

CAUSES & ETIOLOGY:
- Autoimmune reaction against basal keratinocytes
- Associated with Hepatitis C infection (variable association)
- Drug-induced: NSAIDs, antihypertensives, beta-blockers
- Allergic reaction: dental metals (amalgam), contact allergens
- Idiopathic in majority of cases

MOLECULAR MECHANISM:
- T-cell mediated immune response (CD8+ cytotoxic T cells)
- Cytotoxic attack on basal epidermal cells
- Inflammatory cytokine release (TNF-alpha, IFN-gamma)
- Degeneration of basal layer (vacuolar interface dermatitis)
- Apoptosis of keratinocytes

TRIGGERS & EXACERBATING FACTORS:
- Viral infections (especially Hepatitis C)
- Medications (NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors)
- Psychological stress
- Dental materials (amalgam, nickel)
- Trauma to affected areas (Koebner phenomenon)

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS:
- Psoriasis
- Leukoplakia (oral precancerous lesion)
- Lupus erythematosus
- Chronic candidiasis
- Drug eruptions
- Pemphigoid
- Lichen sclerosus

DIAGNOSIS METHODS:
- Clinical examination and morphology
- Skin or mucosal biopsy (confirmatory - essential)
- Direct immunofluorescence (band-like pattern)
- Hepatitis C screening (serologic testing)
- Histopathology: saw-tooth appearance, basal cell degeneration

MONITORING & FOLLOW-UP:
- Assess lesion progression
- Monitor pain and symptom severity
- Regular oral lesion examination (cancer risk)
- Screen for Hepatitis C status
- Assess psychological impact

COMPLICATIONS:
- Chronic pain and discomfort
- Scarring (especially genital and scalp)
- Permanent hair loss (lichen planopilaris)
- Nail destruction and dysfunction
- Increased risk of oral squamous cell carcinoma (1-5%)
- Psychological distress

PATIENT MANAGEMENT ADVICE:
- Avoid identified triggers (medications, irritants)
- Maintain meticulous oral hygiene
- Regular follow-up for oral lesions (cancer surveillance)
- Stress reduction techniques
- Avoid trauma to lesions
- Psychological support for chronic cases

RED FLAGS - EMERGENCY CARE REQUIRED:
- Severe painful oral ulcers preventing eating/drinking
- Rapid worsening of lesions
- Suspicion of malignant transformation
- Severe erosions with bleeding or infection

DISEASE #5: MYCOSIS FUNGOIDES (MF)
Classification: Cutaneous T-Cell Lymphoma / Skin Cancer (CTCL)
Tags: Cancer, Lymphoma, Malignancy, Hematologic
================================================================================

OVERVIEW:
Mycosis fungoides is a rare, chronic cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (skin cancer) arising from malignant proliferation of CD4+ T lymphocytes. Most common type of primary cutaneous lymphoma. Typically progresses slowly through distinct clinical and pathological stages. Not contagious. Can remain localized to skin or progress systemically with involvement of lymph nodes and internal organs in advanced stages.

KEY FACTS:
- Rare cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (primary skin cancer)
- Typically affects older adults (median age 55-60 years)
- More common in males (male:female ratio ~2:1)
- Chronic and progressive disease
- Can remain localized to skin for extended periods
- Systemic progression possible in advanced stages
- Not contagious
- Poor prognosis if visceral involvement develops

SYMPTOMS - EARLY STAGE (PATCH PHASE):
- Pruritic patches (flat, scaly, non-raised lesions)
- Non-blanching erythematous patches
- Common sites: buttocks, breasts, inner thighs, skin folds
- Intense itching (pruritus)
- Often misdiagnosed as eczema or psoriasis
- May persist for years

SYMPTOMS - INTERMEDIATE STAGE (PLAQUE PHASE):
- Infiltrative plaques (raised, thickened lesions)
- Well-defined borders
- Hyperkeratotic (thickened) surface
- Possible skin fold involvement
- Color: violaceous or brownish
- Surface may be scaly or lichenified

SYMPTOMS - ADVANCED STAGE (TUMOR PHASE):
- Nodules and tumors (>1cm, infiltrative)
- Ulceration and bleeding
- Exudation and crusting
- Possible secondary infection
- Erythroderma (whole body redness) in severe cases
- Pain and drainage

SYMPTOMS - SYSTEMIC DISEASE (ADVANCED STAGE IV):
- Lymphadenopathy (enlarged lymph nodes)
- Constitutional B symptoms: fever, night sweats, weight loss
- Hepatomegaly (enlarged liver)
- Splenomegaly (enlarged spleen)
- Organ involvement: visceral lymphomas

SEVERITY & STAGING - TNM CLASSIFICATION:

Stage IA: Limited patches
- <10% body surface area (BSA)
- Normal lymph nodes
- No systemic involvement

Stage IB: Generalized patches
- >10% BSA
- Normal lymph nodes
- No systemic involvement

Stage IIA: Limited plaques
- <10% BSA
- Normal lymph nodes
- No systemic involvement

Stage IIB: Generalized plaques
- >10% BSA
- Normal lymph nodes
- No systemic involvement

Stage III: Erythroderma
- Whole body or near-whole body involvement (>80% BSA)
- Normal lymph nodes
- No systemic disease

Stage IV: Tumor-stage disease with systemic involvement
- Any skin involvement
- Lymph node involvement (N1-3) OR visceral disease (M1)
- Poor prognosis

CAUSES & ETIOLOGY:
- Monoclonal proliferation of malignant T cells (clonal expansion)
- Acquired genetic mutations in T-cell clones
- Loss of normal T-cell regulation and apoptosis
- Etiology not fully understood
- Environmental and genetic factors likely involved
- Possible viral cofactors (HTLV-1 controversial)

MOLECULAR MECHANISM:
- Malignant CD4+ T lymphocytes (helper T cells)
- Loss of normal apoptosis and growth regulation
- Infiltration of epidermis and dermis by neoplastic cells
- Cytokine dysregulation (IL-5, IL-13, TNF-alpha)
- Clonal T-cell receptor rearrangement
- Possible role of immune dysregulation

RISK FACTORS & TRIGGERS:
- Advanced age (peak incidence 50-60 years)
- Male gender
- Genetic predisposition (familial clustering rare)
- Chronic sun exposure (controversial)
- Viral infections (proposed but unproven)
- Chemical exposures (limited evidence)
- Immunosuppression

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS:
- Psoriasis
- Chronic eczema (atopic dermatitis)
- Lichen planus
- Pityriasis rosea
- Drug eruptions
- Inflammatory dermatoses
- Contact dermatitis
- Seborrheic dermatitis

DIAGNOSIS METHODS:
- Clinical evaluation: appearance, morphology, distribution
- Skin biopsy and histopathology (KEY diagnostic tool)
- T-cell clonality testing: PCR for T-cell receptor genes
- Immunophenotyping: flow cytometry (CD4+/CD8+ ratio, surface antigens)
- TNM staging workup:
  - Lymph node examination
  - Imaging studies (CT, PET-CT for advanced disease)
  - Bone marrow biopsy (select cases)
  - Peripheral blood analysis

MONITORING & FOLLOW-UP:
- Regular clinical examination: every 3-6 months
- Skin biopsies if lesions change character or spread
- Lymph node assessment and palpation
- Imaging surveillance (CT/PET) for advanced stages
- Complete blood counts (CBC) if on systemic therapy
- Liver and kidney function tests
- Long-term disease monitoring (potential progression)

COMPLICATIONS:
- Secondary bacterial infections (skin integrity loss)
- Psychological impact: appearance concerns, prognosis anxiety
- Progression to advanced stages (systemic disease)
- Visceral involvement: lymph nodes, liver, spleen, lungs
- Erythroderma with fluid loss and temperature dysregulation
- Leukemic transformation (Sézary syndrome - aggressive variant)
- Treatment-related toxicities
- Increased infection risk (T-cell dysfunction)

PATIENT MANAGEMENT ADVICE:
- Establish regular dermatology follow-up schedule
- Avoid excessive sun exposure
- Carefully monitor skin changes and lesion progression
- Maintain skin care and hygiene
- Psychological support and counseling (highly recommended)
- Avoid scratching (reduces infection risk)
- Report new symptoms promptly
- Seek second opinion at specialized centers
- Participate in patient support groups

RED FLAGS - EMERGENCY CARE REQUIRED:
- Rapid spread of lesions or erythroderma development
- Severe erythroderma with fever/chills (risk of exfoliative dermatitis)
- Signs of systemic disease: lymphadenopathy, hepatomegaly, splenomegaly
- Ulceration with heavy drainage or secondary infection
- B symptoms: fever, night sweats, weight loss (>10% body weight)
- Difficulty with temperature regulation
- Respiratory symptoms (lung involvement)
- New skin ulceration or tumor formation
